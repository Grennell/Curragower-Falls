● About the Falls

What are the Curragower Falls?

Curragower Falls sits in the very heart of Limerick city on the River Shannon, immediately upstream of Sarsfield Bridge, in the shadow of the medieval King John's Castle. It is one of Ireland's finest urban whitewater features — and one of its most unique, because it is both tidal and flow-dependent.

The falls form when the River Shannon drops over a natural rock ledge as the tide ebbs. But unlike a river weir that runs constantly, Curragower needs two things to work: a low tide, and a meaningful release of water from the Ardnacrusha hydroelectric station upstream. When both align, the result is a powerful, dynamic standing wave that has attracted freestyle kayakers and surfers from all over Ireland and beyond.

"It's hard to believe that such an exciting natural feature exists in our very own city centre."
— Get West Adventure

During the winter months — roughly November to April — when the Shannon is running high, the combination of volume and low tide produces the best conditions. Two main waves can form: the Main Gower in the centre of the channel, and Lil' Gower on river right at higher levels. At exceptional flood conditions, Lil' Gower can grow into an impressive six-foot wave.

Curragower isn't just for kayakers. It's a favourite spot for locals walking along Clancy's Strand, photographers, and anyone who loves watching raw water in an extraordinary city setting. The adjacent Curragower Bar is well placed for warming up afterwards.

In 2024, Fergal Lawler of The Cranberries composed a full orchestral piece called "The Curragower Falls", commissioned by RTÉ lyric fm to celebrate its 25th anniversary — a testament to just how deeply these falls are woven into the fabric of Limerick's identity.

● How it works

Tide, Flow & the Falls

Unlike most waterfalls, Curragower is the product of two overlapping forces. Understanding them is the key to knowing when to show up.

01

The Tide Ebbs

As the Shannon tide drops, a head difference builds across the rock ledge. The wave begins to form roughly 2–3 hours before low tide. Use Limerick Dock tides (not Tarbert — they differ).

02

Ardnacrusha Releases

The ESB's Ardnacrusha dam upstream controls how much water flows through. The wave is best when electricity generation is in demand — weekday evenings and cold mornings tend to be most reliable.

03

The Wave Forms

When both factors align, a standing wave emerges mid-channel. At low tide heights of 1.1m or lower the wave goes green; below 0.7m it can be exceptional. The window is roughly 3 hours.

● When to go

Reading the Conditions

Curragower rewards those who do a little homework. Below is a quick guide to what makes a good session — and what doesn't.

✓ Good conditions
  • November to April (higher river levels)
  • Low tide height below 1.1m at Limerick Dock
  • Ideal low tide is 0.7m or under
  • Weekday evenings (peak electricity demand)
  • Cold winter mornings (heating = more generation)
  • 2–3 hours before low tide
  • The Ardnacrusha gates are open / generating
✗ Not ideal
  • Summer (river is low, little generation)
  • Weekend early mornings (low electricity demand)
  • High tide or slack water
  • Low tide above 1.1m
  • During periods of drought or low rainfall
  • Rising tide — wave drops off quickly

Tide tables: Use the Shannon Foynes Port Company tide page and select Limerick Dock. Cross-reference with Irish Times Tides. No official Ardnacrusha release schedule is publicly available, but the local Limerick Kayak Club and paddling community will usually know — check their Facebook group or message board.

The wave rarely works before 8:30 on weekend mornings.
But on a good winter's evening? It's magic.

The falls are often paddled at night too, especially when the city council's car park lights on river left are on. It's one of Limerick's great open secrets.

● For Kayakers & Paddlers

The Two Waves

Curragower is a park-and-play spot through and through. There are slipways both above and below the feature on river right, making access easy. Here's what you'll find depending on conditions.

River Centre

The Main Gower

The headline wave. It sits bang in the middle of the channel and forms as water levels drop toward low tide. Deep and powerful — no fear of power-flipping and hitting the bottom. Most freestyle moves are possible: cartwheels, airwheels, bow stalls, flat spins, enders. A little trickier to get on, but worth it when it's going.

River Right

Lil' Gower

Forms on river right bank at higher water levels. Small and fast, with a nice foam pile at the right level — great for aerial moves and learning new skills. Excellent eddy service makes it beginner-friendly. At exceptional flood levels it can grow to an astonishing 6-foot wave. Only paddled when the main wave is washed out.

Local Club

There is a very strong local club — Limerick Kayak Club — based in the city. If the wave is up, they will know about it. The national freestyle championships have been held on the Gower, drawing paddlers from all over Ireland. The club phone is 086-366-1946 (evenings).

Safety

The wave is deep — the rock ledge is well submerged during active conditions. However, like all moving water, the falls deserve respect. Always paddle with others, especially at higher flows. The Gower is an advanced feature; beginners should paddle with experienced supervision. The Curragower Bar is immediately adjacent for emergency warmth and soup.

● Getting There

Location & Access

📍
Address Clancy's Strand, Limerick City — upstream of Sarsfield Bridge, beneath King John's Castle. The wave is visible from the strand.
🅿️
Parking Park on Clancy's Strand road. Note: the Curragower Bar's car park is private — please do not use it. Parking can be busy during events. River left (opposite bank) car park is lit by council lights on weekday evenings.
🚶
On Foot From Limerick city centre, cross Sarsfield Bridge and turn left — Clancy's Strand is a short riverside walk. The falls are in full view from the strand.
🚤
Slipway Access There are slipways both above and below the feature on river right, giving kayakers easy water access regardless of conditions.
🍺
The Curragower Bar Right beside the falls. Excellent for soup, toasties, and post-paddle warmth. One of Limerick's finest spots.
